💰 Funding & Finance

How money flows into startups and what each investment type means.

What is a Hedge Fund?

A hedge fund is a private investment fund that pools money from wealthy individuals and institutions, then uses aggressive strategies (short selling, leverage, derivatives) to generate returns regardless of whether the market goes up or down. The name "hedge" comes from hedging — protecting against risk.

🔍 Real-World Example

Bridgewater Associates (founded by Ray Dalio) manages ~$120B. If they believe Tesla stock will drop, they can short sell it — borrowing shares, selling them at $200, then buying back at $150, pocketing $50/share. Regular mutual funds can't do this.

Why should you care? Hedge funds sometimes invest in late-stage startups (Series C+). If a hedge fund invests in your startup, it means they see a near-term path to IPO or acquisition.

Stocks & Securities — What Engineers Should Know

Stock = a share of ownership in a company. When you own stock, you own a tiny piece of that company.

Public Stock (e.g., AAPL, GOOG)

  • Traded on stock exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ)
  • Anyone can buy/sell
  • Price set by supply & demand
  • Highly liquid — sell anytime

Private Stock (Startup Equity)

  • NOT traded on exchanges
  • Given to employees as compensation
  • Price set by company valuation
  • Illiquid — can't easily sell
🔍 Real-World Example

Early Uber engineers received stock options at a ~$5M valuation. By IPO, Uber was valued at $82B. An engineer with 0.1% ownership saw their shares worth ~$82M (before dilution). But they couldn't sell any of it until the IPO — that's the "illiquid" part.

Key Financial Instruments

SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

An investor gives you money NOW, gets shares LATER (at the next funding round). No interest, no maturity date. Created by Y Combinator.

Founder-friendly Most common for pre-seed

Convertible Note

A loan that converts to equity later. Has interest rate (typically 2–8%) and a maturity date. If the startup fails, the investor can demand repayment.

More investor-friendly Common in EU/Asia

Priced Round (Series A, B, C...)

Company is formally valued. Investors buy shares at a specific price. Involves lawyers, term sheets, board seats.

Complex $2M+ rounds

Valuation Cap vs. Discount

Cap: Max valuation at which the SAFE converts. Discount: Investor gets shares at e.g. 20% less than the next round's price. Investors get whichever is better for them.

Critical to understand

Idea Stage — "Can this work?"

What you do: Validate the problem exists. Talk to 50+ potential customers. Build nothing yet (or just a landing page).
Team: 1–2 founders
Example: Airbnb started when the founders rented air mattresses in their apartment to conference attendees to test if strangers would pay to sleep in someone's home.

Pre-Seed — "Let's build a prototype"

What you do: Build an MVP (Minimum Viable Product). Get first 10–100 users. Prove people want this.
Funding: $50K–$500K from angels, accelerators (YC, Techstars), or your savings
Key metric: User engagement, waitlist signups
Example: Dropbox made a 3-minute demo video. 75,000 people signed up for the waitlist overnight — that proved demand before writing serious code.

Seed — "We have traction"

What you do: Product-market fit (PMF). Users love it, retention is strong, word-of-mouth is growing.
Funding: $500K–$5M from seed VCs (First Round, Precursor, SV Angel)
Team: 5–15 people
Key metric: MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue), retention rate, NPS
Example: Notion raised a $2M seed round. They had strong user love but were still figuring out go-to-market.

Series A — "Let's scale what works"

What you do: Repeatable growth engine. Hire a real team. Build out sales/marketing.
Funding: $5M–$20M from institutional VCs (a16z, Sequoia, Accel)
Team: 20–50 people
Key metric: Revenue growth rate, unit economics, LTV/CAC ratio
Example: Slack's Series A was $42.8M from Andreessen Horowitz. They had explosive organic growth among dev teams.

Series B — "Dominate the market"

What you do: Scale aggressively. Expand to new markets/geographies. Hire VPs and directors.
Funding: $20M–$60M
Team: 50–200
Example: Figma raised $25M Series B to take on Adobe. They focused on building collaborative features that designers loved.

Series C+ / Late Stage — "Prepare for exit"

What you do: Achieve profitability or clear path to it. Prepare for IPO or acquisition.
Funding: $50M–$500M+ (VCs, hedge funds, PE firms)
Example: Stripe's Series I valued them at $50B. At this stage, it's about global expansion and regulatory compliance.

IPO / Exit — "The liquidity event"

Options: IPO (go public), SPAC, Direct Listing, or Acquisition.
Example: Coinbase did a direct listing on NASDAQ in 2021 at ~$86B valuation. Employees could finally sell their shares.

⚠️ The Valley of Death: ~90% of startups fail. The most dangerous gap is between Seed and Series A. You have some money, some users, but haven't proven you can scale. This is where most startups die.

📊 Equity & Cap Tables

How ownership gets divided — and diluted — as a startup grows.

What is Equity?

Equity = ownership in the company. If you own 10% equity in a company valued at $10M, your stake is worth $1M (on paper). But equity is not cash — you can't spend it until a "liquidity event" (IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale).

Stock Options (What You'll Get as an Employee)

ISOs (Incentive Stock Options)

  • Tax-advantaged (in the US)
  • Must be exercised within 90 days of leaving
  • Subject to AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax)
  • Only for employees

NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options)

  • Taxed as ordinary income on exercise
  • Can be given to contractors, advisors
  • More flexible but less tax-efficient
🔍 Real-World Example: Vesting

You join a startup and get 40,000 options with a 4-year vesting schedule and 1-year cliff. This means: after year 1, you get 10,000 shares (25%). Then ~833 shares vest each month for the remaining 3 years. If you leave after 6 months, you get NOTHING (that's the cliff).

⚠️ Exercise cost matters! If your strike price is $1 and the current value is $10, exercising 10,000 options costs you $10,000 cash but gives you shares worth $100,000. But if the company fails, you lose that $10,000.

Board of Directors — Who Really Controls the Company

The board makes major decisions: hiring/firing the CEO, approving fundraising, approving acquisitions. A typical early-stage board has 3–5 seats.

🔍 Real-World Example: Board Composition After Series A

• Seat 1: Founder/CEO (you)
• Seat 2: Co-founder/CTO
• Seat 3: Lead Series A investor (they demanded a board seat)
• Seat 4: Independent director (experienced exec both sides agree on)
• Seat 5: Seed investor representative

⚠️ If investors have 3 of 5 seats, they can fire you from your own company. This happened to Steve Jobs at Apple in 1985.

Other Important Roles You'll Encounter

VP of Engineering

Manages the engineering org — people, process, delivery. Focuses on HOW things get built. Reports to CTO or CEO.

Product Manager (PM)

Decides WHAT gets built and WHY. Gathers requirements, writes specs, prioritizes the roadmap. Works closely with engineering.

Engineering Manager (EM)

Manages a team of 5–10 engineers. Does 1:1s, performance reviews, removes blockers. Some EMs still code, many don't.

Technical Program Manager (TPM)

Coordinates large cross-team projects. Tracks dependencies, timelines, risks. Essential at 100+ engineer orgs.

DevRel / Developer Advocate

If your product is for developers (API, SDK), DevRel builds community, writes docs, gives talks, and gathers feedback.

Solutions Engineer / Sales Engineer

Technical person who supports the sales team. Demos the product, answers technical questions from prospects, builds proof-of-concepts.

🪜 Engineering Career Ladder

The path from junior to Staff Engineer and beyond — what's expected at each level.

The Two Tracks: IC (Individual Contributor) vs. Management

What Does a Staff Engineer Actually Do?

Staff Engineer is where many engineers aspire to reach. It's the first level where your impact is expected to extend beyond your immediate team.

Scope of Impact by Level

  • Junior: Individual tasks
  • Mid: Features / small projects
  • Senior: Entire team's technical direction
  • Staff: Multiple teams or org-wide systems
  • Principal: Company-wide technical strategy
  • Distinguished: Industry-wide influence

Staff Engineer Archetypes

  • Tech Lead: Guides a team's technical decisions
  • Architect: Designs systems that span teams
  • Solver: Parachutes into the hardest problems
  • Right Hand: CTO's trusted partner for strategy execution
🔍 Real-World Example

A Staff Engineer at Stripe might redesign the entire payment processing pipeline to handle 10x more transactions. This requires coordinating with 5 teams, writing design docs, getting buy-in from leadership, mentoring engineers on the new architecture, and writing the most critical code herself. A Senior Engineer works within a team; a Staff Engineer works across teams.

🔍 Real-World Example: Unit Economics

Your SaaS charges $50/month. Average customer stays 24 months. So LTV = $1,200.
You spend $5,000 on Google Ads and get 20 customers. So CAC = $250.
LTV:CAC ratio = 4.8:1 — this is great! VCs would love this.

But if your churn is 10%/month, customers only stay ~10 months on average, making LTV = $500 and LTV:CAC = 2:1 — now you have a problem to solve before scaling.

⚖️ Legal Basics

Essential legal concepts every founder-engineer should know.

Company Structure

Delaware C-Corp

The standard for VC-backed startups. Almost all US tech startups are Delaware C-Corps because VCs require it, Delaware has well-established corporate law, and it supports preferred stock classes needed for fundraising.

Most common

LLC

Simpler structure, pass-through taxation. Great for bootstrapped businesses, consulting firms, or lifestyle businesses. Most VCs won't invest in an LLC — you'll need to convert to a C-Corp.

Good for bootstrapping

Intellectual Property (IP)

Key IP Concepts

  • Patents: Protect inventions (algorithms, processes). Expensive ($10K–$30K), takes years.
  • Trademarks: Protect brand names, logos. File early — $225–$400.
  • Copyright: Automatic for code you write. But if you wrote it as an employee, your employer owns it.
  • Trade Secrets: Keep it secret (recipes, algorithms). No registration, but must take steps to protect.

Watch Out For

  • IP Assignment: Ensure all founders and employees sign IP assignment agreements — the company must own the code, not individuals.
  • Prior employer IP: If you built something at your previous job, they might claim they own it.
  • Open source licenses: Using GPL code? Your entire codebase might need to be open-sourced. Use MIT/Apache for safety.
  • Non-compete clauses: Check your current employment contract before starting a side project.

Founder Agreements You Need

  • Founders' Agreement: Who owns what, roles, vesting schedules, what happens if someone leaves. GET THIS IN WRITING before writing a line of code.
  • Vesting for founders: Standard is 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff. Yes, even founders should vest — it protects everyone if a co-founder leaves early.
  • 83(b) Election: (US-specific) File this tax form within 30 days of receiving restricted stock. Missing this deadline can cost you hundreds of thousands in taxes.
  • NDAs: Non-Disclosure Agreements. Most VCs won't sign them. Don't ask.

🎯 Your Priority Roadmap

As a software engineer planning a future startup, here's what to learn and when.

Priority Learning Roadmap

CRITICAL NOW
Engineering excellence, system design, product thinking
HIGH
Equity, fundraising, key metrics (MRR, CAC, LTV)
MEDIUM
Legal basics, company structure, IP, term sheets
LEARN LATER
Hedge funds, PE, public markets, advanced finance

Phase 1: While You're Still an Engineer DO NOW

  • Become a great engineer first. Master system design, distributed systems, databases, and shipping production code. This is your superpower as a technical founder.
  • Get to Senior/Staff level. The skills you build (cross-team communication, technical strategy, influencing without authority) are exactly what a CTO needs.
  • Build side projects. Ship something real. Get users. Learn the pain of building a product, not just features.
  • Understand product thinking. Read user research, sit in on PM meetings, think about WHY features exist, not just HOW to build them.
  • Network. Go to meetups, tweet, contribute to open source. Your future co-founder and first investors are in your network.

Phase 2: Preparing to Start 6-12 MONTHS BEFORE

  • Learn fundraising mechanics: SAFEs, term sheets, valuations, dilution
  • Understand unit economics: CAC, LTV, churn, MRR — know these cold
  • Find your co-founder: Ideally someone with complementary skills (you = technical, them = business/sales)
  • Save money: You need 6–12 months of personal runway minimum
  • Validate your idea: Talk to 100+ potential customers. Can you find 10 who would pay for it TODAY?

Phase 3: Actually Launching WHEN YOU START

  • Incorporate properly: Delaware C-Corp, set up equity with vesting, file 83(b)
  • Build the MVP: Not the full vision — the smallest thing that solves the core problem
  • Get to $1M ARR: This is the Series A benchmark. Everything before this is about finding product-market fit
  • Learn to sell: As a technical founder, you MUST learn to sell your product. Nobody else will do it in the early days.
💡 The #1 Advantage of a Technical Founder: You can build the product yourself. While non-technical founders are looking for a CTO and spending their seed round on developers, you're already shipping. This is an enormous advantage — don't underestimate it. Every great dev tool company (GitHub, GitLab, Vercel, Supabase) was started by engineers.
